01 — Water Purification

Boiling

  • Rolling boil for 1 minute at sea level
  • 3 minutes above 6,500 ft elevation
  • Let cool naturally. Do not add ice.

Bleach Disinfection

Use regular unscented household bleach only. No color-safe, no scented, no added cleaners.

Bleach ConcentrationPer GallonPer Liter
6% sodium hypochlorite8 drops (1/8 tsp)2 drops
8.25% sodium hypochlorite6 drops2 drops
  • Mix thoroughly, let stand 30 minutes before drinking
  • Should have a slight chlorine smell after 30 min. If not, repeat dose and wait 15 more minutes.

Solar Disinfection (SODIS)

  1. Use clear PET plastic bottles (1-2 liter soda bottles)
  2. Fill with clear water (pre-filter if cloudy)
  3. Place on dark or reflective surface in direct sunlight
  4. 6 hours in full sun, 2 full days if cloudy
Does NOT work through glass or in shade.

Improvised Filter

Removes particles, NOT pathogens. Always disinfect after filtering.

Layer from bottom to top in a container with a drainage hole:

  1. Gravel (coarse)
  2. Sand (fine)
  3. Charcoal (crushed, from hardwood fire, NOT briquettes)
  4. Sand (fine)
  5. Gravel (coarse)

Signs of Contaminated Water

  • Unusual color, odor, or taste
  • Surface film or foam
  • Blue-green algae (toxic)
  • Proximity to industrial sites, latrines, animal carcasses
  • Stagnant water is higher risk than flowing

02 — Fire Starting

Methods Without Matches

Bow Drill (Friction)

  1. Fireboard: flat, dry softwood (cedar, willow, cottonwood)
  2. Spindle: straight, dry softwood stick, 12-18 inches, thumb-width
  3. Bow: curved stick with cordage (shoelace, paracord)
  4. Socket: hardwood or stone with depression to hold spindle top
  5. Cut a V-notch in fireboard with depression. Place tinder bundle below notch.
  6. Wrap cordage around spindle, press socket on top, saw bow back and forth
  7. Hot coal forms in notch. Transfer to tinder bundle, blow gently.

Flint and Steel / Ferrocerium Rod

  • Strike steel against flint at 30-degree angle toward tinder
  • Direct sparks into char cloth or fine tinder

Battery and Steel Wool

  • Touch both terminals of a 9V battery to fine (#0000) steel wool
  • Ignites immediately. Have tinder ready.

Magnifying Lens

  • Focus sunlight to a pinpoint on dark tinder
  • Works with eyeglasses, binocular lens, bottom of soda can (polished), or clear ice shaped as lens

Tinder (catches spark)

Char cloth, dryer lint, cotton balls with petroleum jelly, birch bark, dry grass, cattail fluff, fatwood shavings, fine wood shavings, dried pine needles

Kindling (feeds small flame)

Pencil-thickness dry sticks, small split wood, cardboard strips. Must be bone-dry.

03 — First Aid Essentials

CPR (Adult)

  1. 30 compressions : 2 breaths (30:2 ratio)
  2. Rate: 100-120 per minute
  3. Depth: at least 2 inches, no more than 2.4 inches
  4. Hand placement: center of chest, lower half of breastbone
  5. Allow full chest recoil between compressions
  6. If untrained: hands-only CPR (continuous compressions, no breaths)

Children/infants: Same 30:2 (one rescuer), 15:2 (two rescuers). Infant compressions: 2 fingers, 1.5 inches deep.

Choking (Heimlich) -- Conscious Adult

  1. Stand behind person, wrap arms around waist
  2. Make a fist, place thumb side against abdomen above navel, below ribcage
  3. Grasp fist with other hand
  4. Quick inward-and-upward thrusts
  5. Repeat until object is expelled or person becomes unconscious
  6. If unconscious: lower to ground, begin CPR, check mouth before each breath

Choking Infant (<1 year)

  1. Face down on forearm, head lower than chest
  2. 5 back blows between shoulder blades with heel of hand
  3. Flip face up, 5 chest thrusts with 2 fingers on breastbone
  4. Alternate until clear

Tourniquet Application

When to use: Life-threatening extremity bleeding that cannot be controlled by direct pressure.

  1. Place 2-3 inches above wound (never on a joint)
  2. Pull band tight and secure through buckle
  3. Twist windlass rod until bleeding stops
  4. Lock windlass in clip/holder
  5. Write the time on tourniquet or patient's forehead ("TQ" + time)
  6. Do NOT loosen or remove once applied
Improvised tourniquet: Use 1.5+ inch wide fabric (belt, torn shirt) with a stick as windlass. Never use wire, rope, or narrow material.

Wound Care

  1. Apply direct pressure with clean cloth for 10-15 minutes
  2. Irrigate wound with clean water under pressure (squeeze bottle)
  3. Remove visible debris
  4. Apply antibiotic ointment if available
  5. Cover with sterile dressing, bandage in place
  6. Change dressing every 24 hours or when wet/dirty
Infection signs: Increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pus, red streaks from wound, fever.

Burns

DegreeAppearanceAction
1st (superficial)Red, painful, no blistersCool running water 10-20 min, aloe, OTC pain relief
2nd (partial)Blisters, wet, very painfulCool water 10-20 min, do NOT pop blisters, loosely cover
3rd (full)White/charred, may be painlessCover loosely, seek emergency care immediately
Never use ice, butter, toothpaste, or adhesive bandages on burns. Chemical burns: flush with water for 20+ minutes.

Splinting Fractures

  1. Immobilize the joint ABOVE and BELOW the fracture
  2. Pad the splint (sticks, boards, rolled magazines)
  3. Secure with cloth strips, tape, belts. Snug but not tight.
  4. Check circulation below splint: pulse, feeling, color, warmth
  5. Do NOT attempt to straighten or reset the bone

Recognizing Stroke -- FAST

FaceAsk to smile. Does one side droop?
ArmsRaise both arms. Does one drift down?
SpeechRepeat a phrase. Is speech slurred?
TimeNote exact time symptoms started. Call 911.

Heart Attack Signs

  • Chest pain/pressure/squeezing lasting more than a few minutes
  • Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, neck, back, or stomach
  • Shortness of breath, cold sweat, nausea, lightheadedness
  • Women may have atypical symptoms: fatigue, indigestion, anxiety
Action: Have person chew 1 regular aspirin (325 mg) or 4 baby aspirin (81 mg each) if not allergic. Call 911.

Shock Treatment

Signs: Pale/cool/clammy skin, rapid weak pulse, rapid shallow breathing, confusion, thirst.

  1. Lay person flat on back
  2. Elevate legs 8-12 inches (unless head/neck/spine/leg injury)
  3. Cover with blanket to maintain warmth
  4. Do NOT give food or drink
  5. Monitor breathing. Be prepared to begin CPR.

04 — Knots

Bowline -- fixed loop that won't slip

Use: Rescue loops, anchoring, securing loads

1. Form a small loop ("the rabbit hole") in the standing part 2. Pass the free end UP through the loop (rabbit comes out of hole) 3. Route free end BEHIND the standing part (around the tree) 4. Pass free end back DOWN through the small loop (back in the hole) 5. Tighten by pulling standing part while holding the free end
"The rabbit comes out of the hole, goes around the tree, and goes back in the hole."

Square Knot -- joins two ropes of equal diameter

Use: Bandages, bundling

1. Hold one end in each hand 2. Right over left, tuck under 3. Left over right, tuck under 4. Tighten evenly
"Right over left, left over right." Both free ends should exit on the SAME side.

Clove Hitch -- quick attachment to a post or pole

Use: Starting lashings, temporary tie-off

1. Wrap rope around post 2. Cross over the first wrap 3. Tuck free end under the second wrap (under the X) 4. Pull tight
Can slip under variable load. Secure with a half-hitch for safety.

Taut-Line Hitch -- adjustable tension loop

Use: Tent guy lines, adjustable tie-downs

1. Wrap rope around anchor (stake, tree) 2. Pass free end inside the loop: two wraps toward the anchor 3. One more wrap outside (above) the first two, going away from anchor 4. Pass free end through this last loop 5. Slide to adjust tension. Holds under load.

Trucker's Hitch -- mechanical advantage for tight tie-downs

Use: Securing loads to vehicles, clotheslines, tarps

1. Tie a fixed loop in the standing part (use a slip knot) 2. Route free end around anchor point (hook, stake) 3. Thread free end back through the loop -- creates a 3:1 pulley 4. Pull tight 5. Secure with two half-hitches against the loop
Gives roughly 3:1 mechanical advantage.

Figure-Eight -- stopper knot

Use: Climbing, preventing rope from pulling through

1. Make a loop in the rope 2. Pass free end around behind the standing part 3. Thread free end through the original loop 4. Result looks like the number 8
For a figure-eight loop: tie figure-eight, pass free end around anchor, retrace the path back through.

05 — Shelter

Site Selection

  • Avoid: Hilltops (wind/lightning), valley floors (cold air, flooding), dead trees, dry riverbeds
  • Seek: Natural windbreaks, south-facing slopes, near water but above flood line
  • Check above for "widow-makers" (dead hanging branches)

Basic Lean-To

  1. Place a ridgepole (8-12 ft) between two trees at chest height
  2. Lean branches at 45-60 degree angle against the ridgepole on the windward side
  3. Layer debris from bottom to top (like shingles): leaves, pine boughs, bark, grass
  4. Minimum 2-3 feet thick debris layer for rain protection
  5. Build a bed of dry leaves/pine needles 4-6 inches thick on the ground
  6. Orient opening away from prevailing wind, ideally toward a fire

Insulation Principles

Ground insulation is #1 priority. The ground steals body heat 25x faster than air.
  • Use leaves, pine needles, grass, cardboard, foam, newspapers
  • Dead air space = insulation. Smaller shelter = easier to heat with body warmth.
  • In snow: body heat can warm an enclosed snow shelter to 32F even in subzero conditions

Emergency Materials

Trash bagsCut open for poncho or rain fly. Stuff with leaves for insulation.
Space blankets (mylar)Reflective side facing body retains ~90% of radiated body heat
TarpsA-frame or lean-to configuration with paracord
VehicleStay with your vehicle in most situations. Visible and provides shelter.
SnowQuinzhee (packed snow mound, hollowed out) or snow trench with branches/tarp cover

06 — Signaling for Help

Universal Distress Signals

3 of anything = distress. 3 whistle blasts, 3 fires, 3 gunshots, 3 flashes. Pause between sets and repeat.

A whistle carries farther than your voice and requires less energy.

Ground-to-Air Signals

Make at least 10 ft tall. Use contrast with ground (rocks, logs, trampled snow, clothing).

SymbolMeaning
VNeed assistance
XNeed medical help
INeed supplies
→ (arrow)Traveling this direction
LLAll is well

Signal Mirror

  1. Hold mirror near face, extend opposite hand toward target (aircraft, rescuers)
  2. Reflect light onto your extended hand
  3. Adjust angle so reflected light flashes across your fingers toward target
  4. Sweep slowly back and forth
A signal mirror can be seen up to 50 miles in clear conditions. Any reflective surface works: CD, phone screen, polished metal, foil.

Signal Fire

  • 3 fires in a triangle (~100 ft apart) = international distress signal
  • Green branches/leaves for white smoke (visible against dark backgrounds)
  • Oil, rubber, or plastic for black smoke (visible against snow/clouds)

07 — Navigation

Stick Shadow Method (most reliable)

  1. Place a straight stick (3+ ft) vertically in the ground on flat, sunny terrain
  2. Mark the tip of the shadow with a stone. This is West.
  3. Wait 15-30 minutes
  4. Mark the new shadow tip. This is East.
  5. Draw a line between the two marks: this is your East-West line
  6. Stand with the first mark (W) on your left, second mark (E) on your right
  7. You are facing North (Northern Hemisphere)

North Star (Polaris)

  1. Find the Big Dipper (Ursa Major)
  2. The two stars forming the outer edge of the "cup" are pointer stars
  3. Extend a line from these two stars about 5x their spacing
  4. Points to Polaris (last star in the Little Dipper's handle)
  5. Polaris indicates True North (within ~1 degree)

Watch Method (analog)

Northern Hemisphere: Point the hour hand at the sun. Bisect the angle between the hour hand and 12 o'clock. That line points roughly South.

Southern Hemisphere: Point 12 o'clock at the sun. Bisect the angle between 12 and the hour hand. That line points roughly North.

Adjust for Daylight Saving Time (use position where 1 o'clock would be standard time).

Basic Map Reading

  • Top of map = North (unless otherwise marked)
  • Contour lines close together = steep. Far apart = gentle slope.
  • V's pointing uphill = valley. Pointing downhill = ridge.
  • Blue = water, green = vegetation, brown = contour lines, black = man-made
  • 1:24,000 scale (common USGS): 1 inch = 2,000 feet (~0.4 miles)

08 — Vehicle Emergencies

Jump Starting a Car

  1. Position cars so batteries are close. Vehicles must NOT touch.
  2. Turn off both engines and all accessories
  3. Identify positive (+) and negative (-) terminals on both batteries
  4. RED cable to DEAD battery positive (+)
  5. RED cable other end to GOOD battery positive (+)
  6. BLACK cable to GOOD battery negative (-)
  7. BLACK cable other end to unpainted metal on dead car's engine block (NOT the dead battery negative)
  8. Start the working vehicle, let run 2-3 minutes
  9. Start the dead vehicle
  10. Remove cables in REVERSE order (7, 6, 5, 4)
  11. Drive the revived vehicle for at least 15-20 minutes
Step 7 is critical. Grounding to the engine block avoids sparks near the battery, which can release flammable hydrogen gas.

Changing a Tire

  1. Pull to flat, stable surface. Turn on hazards.
  2. Apply parking brake. Chock opposite tires (rocks, wood).
  3. Loosen lug nuts 1/2 turn BEFORE jacking (counterclockwise)
  4. Place jack on vehicle's designated jack point (check owner's manual)
  5. Raise until flat tire is ~6 inches off ground
  6. Remove lug nuts, remove flat tire
  7. Mount spare, hand-tighten lug nuts in a star/cross pattern
  8. Lower until tire touches ground but doesn't bear full weight
  9. Fully tighten lug nuts in star pattern
  10. Lower completely, remove jack
Compact/donut spares: Max speed 50 mph, max distance 50-70 miles.

Stranded in Vehicle

  • Stay with the vehicle (easier to spot than a person on foot)
  • Run engine 10-15 minutes per hour for heat. Crack window slightly for ventilation.
  • Ensure tailpipe is clear of snow/mud/debris
  • Tie bright cloth to antenna or hang from window
  • Dome light on at night
  • In snow: stomp out large SOS or HELP near vehicle
  • Phone: airplane mode, text instead of call (uses less power)

Universal Edibility Test (last resort)

Do NOT use for mushrooms. No safe field test exists for wild mushrooms.
  1. Test one plant part at a time (leaf, stem, root, fruit separately)
  2. Fast for 8 hours before testing
  3. Skin: Crush and rub on inner wrist. Wait 15 minutes.
  4. Lip: Touch to outer lip. Wait 15 minutes.
  5. Tongue: Place on tongue. Wait 15 minutes. Spit out.
  6. Chew: Chew and hold in mouth 15 minutes. Do NOT swallow. Spit out.
  7. If no reaction: swallow a small amount. Wait 8 hours.
  8. If still fine: eat a small handful. Wait another 8 hours.

Avoid plants with: milky/discolored sap, beans/seeds in pods, bitter/soapy taste, almond scent, thorns on leaves, "three-leaf" pattern.

10 — Weather

Reading Clouds

Cloud TypeWhat It Means
Cirrus (thin, wispy, high)Fair now. Weather change in 24-48 hrs.
Cumulus (white, puffy)Fair if small. Watch if growing tall.
Cumulonimbus (tall, dark, anvil)Thunderstorms, hail, tornadoes. Seek shelter.
Stratus (gray blanket, low)Drizzle, overcast. Generally not severe.
Nimbostratus (dark, thick, low)Steady rain or snow for hours.
Lenticular (lens-shaped, near mountains)Very high winds. Do not go higher.

Other Indicators

  • Rapidly falling barometric pressure = storm approaching
  • Wind shifting counterclockwise (N. Hemisphere) = weather deteriorating
  • Ring around moon/sun = rain/snow within 24-48 hrs

Lightning Safety -- 30/30 Rule

If time between flash and thunder is 30 seconds or less (~6 miles), seek shelter. Stay sheltered until 30 minutes after last thunder.
  • Safest: enclosed building or hard-topped vehicle
  • Avoid: tall isolated trees, hilltops, open water, metal fences, open fields
  • If caught in open: crouch low on balls of feet, minimize ground contact, cover ears
  • If someone is struck: safe to touch immediately. Begin CPR if needed.

Tornado Safety

  • Warning signs: dark/green sky, large hail, loud roar (freight train), rotating wall cloud
  • Shelter: lowest interior room (bathroom, closet), away from windows, under sturdy furniture
  • In a vehicle: do NOT outrun. Drive perpendicular to path. If trapped, park, seatbelt on, duck below windows.
  • Mobile home: evacuate to sturdy structure or low ground

Hurricane Safety

  • Evacuate if ordered
  • Fill bathtub with water before storm (flushing/cleaning)
  • The eye is calm but the storm will resume. Stay sheltered.
  • After: avoid floodwater and downed power lines

13 — Sanitation

Emergency Bucket Toilet

  1. Line a 5-gallon bucket with heavy-duty trash bag
  2. Place pool noodle or pipe insulation on rim for a seat (or snap-on toilet seat lid)
  3. After each use: cover waste with kitty litter, sawdust, wood ash, or dirt
  4. When bag is 2/3 full, tie off securely and dispose properly
  5. Keep hand sanitizer or handwashing station immediately adjacent

Cat Hole (outdoor, short-term)

  • Dig hole 6-8 inches deep
  • At least 200 feet from water sources, trails, and camp
  • Cover with original soil
  • Pack out toilet paper in a sealed bag (or burn carefully)

Waste Disposal

  • Never dump waste in or near water sources
  • Bury human waste at least 200 ft (70 paces) from water
  • Store trash away from living area
  • Dead animals: bury at least 3 feet deep, 200+ feet from water and camp

Hand Hygiene Without Running Water

Improvised Handwashing Station

  • Poke small hole near bottom of a water jug. Plug with tape/twig. Release to create trickle, lather with soap.
  • Or: fill a large Ziploc bag, poke small hole in bottom corner

Without Soap

  • Wood ash + water creates a mild lye soap
  • Sand works as an abrasive scrub (rinse after)
  • Hand sanitizer (60%+ alcohol) kills germs but does NOT remove dirt
  • 20 seconds minimum washing time

14 — Power Outage

Generator Safety

NEVER run a generator indoors, in a garage, or in any enclosed space.
Carbon monoxide (CO) is colorless and odorless. It kills within minutes.
  • Place generator at least 20 feet from the house, exhaust away from windows/doors
  • Install battery-powered CO detectors on every level
  • CO symptoms: headache, dizziness, nausea, confusion, loss of consciousness
  • If CO suspected: get everyone outside immediately, call 911
  • Let generator cool before refueling
  • Never connect generator directly to house wiring (can kill utility workers). Use a transfer switch or plug devices directly.

Battery Conservation

  • Phone: airplane mode, minimum brightness, close background apps, disable Bluetooth/Wi-Fi/location
  • Turn phone off completely when not using it
  • Airplane mode can stretch battery to 4-7 days with minimal use
  • Text messages use less power than voice calls
  • Car battery: charge USB devices via 12V port. Run engine 10-15 min.

Alternative Lighting

HeadlampsPreferable to flashlights (hands-free)
CandlesNever leave unattended. Stable holder on nonflammable surface.
Cooking oil lampHeat-safe container + cotton wick resting on rim
Shortening candlePush cotton wick into open can of Crisco. Burns for hours.
Glow sticks8-12 hours. Safe around children, no fire risk.
Solar garden lightsBring inside at night, recharge outside during the day

Food Preservation During Outage

  • Refrigerator: keeps food cold ~4 hours if door stays closed
  • Full freezer: holds temperature ~48 hours. Half-full ~24 hours.
  • Do NOT open doors unnecessarily
  • Move food to coolers with ice if outage will be prolonged
  • Frozen food with ice crystals or at 40F or below can be safely refrozen
